Blog Entry - 4/29/2022 - Is flying safer than driving?

Everyone has heard "Flying is safer than driving," usually from a news source. Have you ever wondered if it was true? Instinctively, flying seems more risky than driving, but that isn't a logical way to compare. I was curious, so, I looked into the articles that make the safety claim. The studies I read compare one of two things: Overall risk in your life or risk per mile.
That makes sense if you compare driving across the US to flying across the US. And, the lifetime risk is not a way to compare the risk of a specific event. "Flying is safer than driving" makes it sound like every time you get into a car, you are taking a bigger risk than every time you get into a plane.
To check that aspect of the safety claim, we should compare the number of fatalities to the number of trips.

Flying risk

According to a Reuters article (source 1 below), there were .27 fatal accidents per million flights. Let's make that whole numbers, so 27 fatal crashes / 100,000,000 flights. That's pretty low.
It also says that in 2020 there were 5 fatal crashes of commercial airplanes totaling 299 deaths, so 60 deaths each. We should multiply crashes by the number of deaths to get the per person risk.
27 flights * 60 deaths = 1,620 deaths / 100,000,000 flights.

Driving risk

According to the NHTSA (source 2 below), there were 38,824 deaths in the US in 2020.
According to Energy.gov (source 3 below), there were 366,000,000,000 trips in the US in 2020.
So, that’s 38,824 deaths / 366,000,000,000 trips. (per person risk is already built-in here because this was the number of deaths, not the number of crashes)

Comparison

Let's compare 1 billion trips for each mode of travel.
Flight: 16,200 deaths / 1 billion trips
Ground travel: 106 deaths / 1 billion trips
That makes getting on a plane about 150x riskier than getting in a car for any random trip of any length.

Caveat

Yes, I did compare the rates of cars in the US to the rates of flights on the globe, but I am comparing rates, not totals. So, that is only unfair if the car fatality rate in the US is far, far higher than the rate for the whole planet. I don't have what I would consider reliable numbers for this, so this is a little shaky, but from what I have seen, Europe is lower on-road fatality rates, but India and China are higher, so the US might be a little low, on average.

Conclusion

Method comparison:

  • Per mile: Flight is safer
  • Lifetime risk: Flight is safer (especially if you never get on a plane in your life)
  • Per trip: Driving is safer

Does this mean any particular person should fear getting onto a plane? The risk is still very low. So, they shouldn't fear getting on a plane. However, it doesn't look like the blanket statement "flying is safer than driving" holds up from every angle.
It certainly looks like a commercial pilot is at a much higher risk than a cabbie, but that is unlikely to surprise anyone.
